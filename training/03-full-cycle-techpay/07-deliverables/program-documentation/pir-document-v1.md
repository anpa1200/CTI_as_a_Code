> It contains generic questions with no stakeholder buy-in, no collection tasks, no

> what you are replacing — and why a real PIR framework looks completely different.

---

## Purpose

This document establishes the Priority Intelligence Requirements (PIRs) for the
TechPay information security function. These requirements will guide the collection
and analysis of threat intelligence to support security decision-making.

---

## Priority Intelligence Requirements

**PIR 1:** What are the main threats to the financial sector?

**PIR 2:** What malware and ransomware are targeting organizations like TechPay?

**PIR 3:** Are there any known vulnerabilities in our technology stack that are being
actively exploited?

**PIR 4:** What is the current threat landscape for payment card data theft?

> **What is wrong with this PIR document (training analysis):**
>
> 1. **No stakeholders beyond "the CISO"** — no SOC, no DE, no IR, no legal, no business
> 2. **Questions are generic, not organization-specific** — PIR 1 could apply to any financial entity on Earth
> 3. **No "so that" framing** — a PIR must state the decision it informs; these don't
> 4. **No SIRs (Specific Intelligence Requirements)** — no collection tasks, no sources
> 5. **No production cadence** — who produces what, when, for whom?
> 6. **No confidence thresholds** — what constitutes an actionable answer?
> 7. **No feedback loop** — how does the consumer tell the analyst if the product was useful?
> 8. **Two-year-old document never reviewed** — this tells you about organizational CTI maturity
